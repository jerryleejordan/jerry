Ten Mental Health Disorders

07 Aug 2023

List by Prevalence

  1. Depression: A mood disorder characterised by persistent feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and loss of interest in activities. It can affect daily functioning and physical health.

  2. Anxiety Disorders: A group of disorders, including generalised anxiety, panic disorder, and social anxiety, marked by excessive worry, fear, and avoidance behaviours that impact daily life.

  3. Schizophrenia: A severe disorder involving distorted thoughts, hallucinations, delusions, and impaired emotional responses. It often impairs one’s ability to differentiate between reality and imagination.

  4. Bipolar Disorder: Individuals experience extreme mood swings, cycling between manic episodes of high energy and depressive episodes of low mood. These shifts can disrupt daily routines.

  5. Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD): Involves unwanted, repetitive thoughts (obsessions) and rituals or actions (compulsions) that are performed to alleviate anxiety. These patterns can consume a significant amount of time.

  6. Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Occurs after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. Symptoms include flashbacks, nightmares, hypervigilance, and emotional numbness.

  7. Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD): Commonly diagnosed in childhood, it involves difficulty sustaining attention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. It can impact academic and social functioning.

  8. Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD): Characterised by unstable relationships, intense mood swings, impulsivity, and a distorted self-image. Individuals may struggle with self-worth and have difficulty regulating emotions.

  9. Eating Disorders: Including anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and binge-eating disorder, these conditions involve unhealthy relationships with food, body image, and weight.

  10. Substance Use Disorders: Involves the misuse of substances like alcohol, drugs, or medications, leading to negative consequences on physical health, relationships, and overall functioning.

Note: These summaries are very concise and mental disorders are complex conditions that can manifest differently in each individual. It’s important to consult mental health professionals for accurate assessment and treatment.

Counselling in New Zealand

06 Aug 2023

Supporting Mental Health and Well-Being

Counselling plays a crucial role in promoting mental health and well-being in New Zealand. As a diverse and multicultural nation, New Zealand recognises the importance of addressing the unique challenges faced by its population, making counselling services widely accessible and tailored to meet individual needs.

Mental Health Care New Zealand

New Zealand places a strong emphasis on mental health care, with a commitment to reducing the stigma surrounding seeking help for emotional and psychological issues. The country has a robust mental health framework that includes both public and private counselling services. Access to government-funded services, such as free helplines and community-based counselling, ensures that help is available to those who may otherwise struggle to afford it.

Counselling Professionals New Zealand

Counselling in New Zealand is provided by a range of professionals, including registered psychologists, counsellors, and social workers. These practitioners are highly trained and adhere to ethical guidelines to ensure quality and safe therapeutic practices. Additionally, cultural competency and sensitivity are emphasised to accommodate the diverse backgrounds and unique needs of clients, including Māori and Pacific Island communities.

Early Intervention and Preventative Mental Health Care

In recent years, there has been a growing recognition of the importance of early intervention and preventative mental health care in New Zealand. Schools and workplaces often offer counselling support and mental health programs to foster well-being and resilience. This proactive approach helps address mental health challenges before they escalate, contributing to a healthier and more productive society.

Digital Counselling in New Zealand

New Zealand has also embraced digital innovations in counselling services. Telehealth and online counselling platforms have become increasingly popular, particularly in rural and remote areas where access to face-to-face services may be limited.

Counselling New Zealand

Counselling in New Zealand is a vital component of the country’s comprehensive mental health care system. With a focus on accessibility, cultural sensitivity, and early intervention, counselling services play a crucial role in supporting the mental health and well-being of New Zealanders, fostering a more resilient and thriving society.

Compassion-Focused Therapy for Couples

06 Aug 2023

Couples Counselling with CFT

Compassion-Focused Therapy (CFT), a therapeutic approach rooted in cultivating compassion and empathy, has proven to be a valuable tool in couples counselling. Recognising that relationships can be challenging and emotionally charged, CFT offers a compassionate and understanding framework to address conflict and foster emotional connection between partners.

Compassion-Focused Therapy Process for Couples

In couples counselling using Compassion-Focused Therapy, the focus is on developing self-compassion and compassion for the partner. Couples are encouraged to explore their emotional experiences with kindness and empathy, which can help reduce self-criticism and promote a more supportive and understanding environment.

Key Techniques in CFT for Couples

The key techniques of Compassion-Focused Therapy used in couples counselling include:

  1. Developing Emotional Awareness: Couples are guided to identify and express their emotions in a non-judgmental and compassionate manner. Emotional awareness helps partners better understand their feelings and respond to each other’s emotions with empathy.

  2. Cultivating Self-Compassion: CFT emphasises the importance of self-compassion for each individual in the relationship. As partners develop self-compassion, they become better equipped to manage their own emotional responses and communicate their needs effectively.

  3. Empathy and Active Listening: Couples are encouraged to actively listen to each other’s experiences and perspectives with empathy and understanding. By practicing empathy, partners can build a deeper connection and create a safe space for emotional expression.

  4. Compassionate Communication: CFT emphasises the use of compassionate language and communication. Couples are encouraged to express their feelings and needs in a gentle and empathetic manner, avoiding blame or criticism.

  5. Joint Mindfulness Practices: Couples may engage in joint mindfulness exercises to enhance emotional regulation and attunement to each other’s needs. Mindfulness practices promote present-moment awareness and can help partners respond to each other with compassion and understanding.

Couples Counselling Outcomes with CFT

Compassion-Focused Therapy in couples counselling fosters a culture of emotional safety and support within the relationship. By cultivating compassion for oneself and the partner, couples can navigate challenges with kindness and empathy, fostering a more nurturing and fulfilling partnership. CFT provides a transformative approach to couples counselling, helping partners build a foundation of emotional connection and mutual understanding.

Compassion-Focused Therapy

05 Aug 2023

CFT Therapeutic Approach

Compassion-Focused Therapy (CFT) is a therapeutic approach that emphasises the cultivation of compassion and kindness, both towards oneself and others, as a means of promoting mental and emotional well-being. Developed by psychologist Paul Gilbert, CFT draws from evolutionary psychology, attachment theory, and neuroscience to help individuals address issues related to shame, self-criticism, and emotional difficulties.

Theories of Evolution in CFT

At the core of Compassion-Focused Therapy is the recognition that many mental health challenges arise from the human brain’s evolved mechanisms, such as threat detection and self-protection. However, in modern society, these mechanisms can sometimes become maladaptive and lead to excessive self-criticism, feelings of inadequacy, and emotional distress.

Creating a Compassionate Mindset in CFT

The primary goal of CFT is to foster a compassionate mindset that encourages individuals to treat themselves and others with kindness, warmth, and understanding. The therapist guides clients in developing self-compassion, which involves being understanding and gentle with oneself during times of difficulty or perceived failure.

Techniques and Exercises in CFT

CFT utilises various techniques and exercises to facilitate the development of compassion, such as imagery-based practices that evoke feelings of warmth and caring towards oneself and others. These practices help individuals regulate their emotions, soothe their stress responses, and build a sense of emotional resilience.

The Compassionate mind in CFT

Another important aspect of CFT is the exploration of the compassionate mind’s three components: the soothing system (associated with compassion and safety), the drive system (linked to motivation and achievement), and the threat system (related to self-protection and survival). The therapist helps individuals balance these systems to promote well-being and reduce emotional suffering.

Treating Mental Health with CFT

Compassion-Focused Therapy has shown promise in treating various mental health conditions, including depression, anxiety, trauma, and eating disorders. By fostering self-compassion, individuals can break free from cycles of self-criticism and develop healthier relationships with themselves and others.

CFT with Individuals and in Groups

CFT can be conducted in individual or group therapy settings, and its compassionate approach creates a safe space for individuals to explore their emotions, experiences, and self-perceptions. Through cultivating compassion, clients can build a more compassionate and fulfilling relationship with themselves and the world around them.
